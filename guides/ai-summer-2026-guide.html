What even IS Artificial Intelligence?

Before we talk about activities, here is a quick, honest explanation of the tools in this guide. Read this first — it takes 3 minutes.

🧠

AI Chatbots (Claude, ChatGPT)

Programs trained on billions of written texts. You type something in plain English, it replies. Not a search engine — it generates responses, writes stories, explains concepts, and helps with problems.

"Write me a calm bedtime story about a train for my 6-year-old" → it writes the whole story in seconds.
🎨

AI Image Generators

You describe a picture in words and the tool draws it. No art skills needed. Brilliant for creative kids who want to see their imagination become a real image instantly.

"A friendly dragon reading a book in a library, children's illustration style" → a real picture appears.
🎙️

AI Voice Tools

Type text, get a spoken voice reading it out. Used for podcasts, audiobooks, and videos. Great for older children making content, and for reading stories aloud to younger ones.

Type a story → AI reads it aloud in a calm, natural voice → play it back as an audiobook.
💻

AI Coding Helpers

You describe what you want an app or game to do in plain English. AI writes the code. Your child does not need to know programming — they just explain their idea clearly.

"Make a quiz app that asks me 10 questions about Pakistan's history" → it builds the whole app.
📚

AI Tutors (Khanmigo)

Like a patient tutor available any time, day or night. Your child asks a question, the AI explains at their level — and never makes them feel silly for asking the same thing twice.

"Explain fractions to me like I'm 8 years old" → it adjusts the language to match their age.
⚠️

What AI is NOT

AI is not always right. It can make things up and sound very confident. It doesn't have feelings, opinions, or life experience. Teaching kids to fact-check AI is just as important as using it.

Always check important facts. AI is a starting point, not the final word. This is a lesson worth repeating.

Ask an Animal Expert

Your child picks their favourite animal and asks the AI five questions about it. Then you look up the same answers in a book or Wikipedia. Did the AI get it right? This critical thinking habit is the most important digital skill of 2026.

Step by step
  1. Open claude.ai and let your child type: "Tell me 5 amazing facts about [animal]"
  2. Read the answers together. Write them down on paper
  3. Open wikipedia.org and search the same animal
  4. Go through each fact together. Correct or tick each one
  5. Score the AI out of 5. Ask: "Why do you think it got that one wrong?"
  6. Repeat with a different animal next time — track their scores week by week
Why this matters: AI sounds confident even when it's wrong. Building the habit of checking early is one of the most protective things you can do for your child in the age of AI-generated information.

🌈 AI Story Activities for Children with ASD

This section is for parents of children on the autism spectrum. All activities are free, use familiar tools, and are designed around predictability, special interests, and sensory comfort. No tech experience needed.

ASD-FriendlyAges 5–13100% Free

What is a Social Story — and why does AI make it easier?

A Social Story is a short, calm, first-person narrative that describes a situation a child finds confusing or stressful. It was developed in 1991 by educator Carol Gray specifically for children with autism. The story describes what will happen, why, and what the child can do — removing uncertainty so the child feels prepared rather than overwhelmed.

Before AI, parents and therapists had to write each Social Story by hand, finding the right words, tone, and structure for each individual child and situation. With Claude, you can generate a personalised Social Story in under 60 seconds, then tweak it for your child's exact vocabulary, favourite characters, and specific anxiety triggers.

📖 Example situations for Social Stories: First day at a new school · Going to the barber/hairdresser · Waiting in a queue · Eating at a restaurant · Meeting a new relative · Going to the doctor · Attending a birthday party · Using a public toilet · Starting a new routine
Activity 1Ages 5–13⏱ 30–45 min

Personalised Story Series Around Their Special Interest

Children with ASD often have one or two topics they love intensely — trains, Minecraft, dinosaurs, a particular cartoon character, space, numbers. This activity puts that special interest at the centre of every story. Familiarity and repetition reduce anxiety, while the story format quietly builds language, sequencing, and emotional recognition skills.

Phase 1: Set up (first session only)
1
Identify the special interest

What does your child talk about most? What do they reach for when they're stressed or need to self-regulate? That is your starting point. Examples: trains, a specific Minecraft character, a dinosaur species, a number sequence, a specific TV show character.

2
Create a free account on Claude

Go to claude.ai. Click Sign Up. Enter your email. Click the verification link. You now have a free account. This takes about 2 minutes.

3
Write a "setup prompt" you can reuse every session

Copy the prompt below, fill in the blanks, and save it in your notes app. Use this every session — the consistency of the opening prompt helps establish a predictable routine.

Your reusable prompt template — save this
"Write a short, calm story for a [age]-year-old child with autism who loves [special interest]. Use very short sentences. No sudden surprises or scary events. The main character faces a small problem and solves it calmly. The character feels safe and happy at the end. Use simple vocabulary. The story should take about 2 minutes to read aloud."
Phase 2: Each story session
4
Paste the prompt into Claude and generate the story

Open a new Claude chat. Paste your saved prompt. Click send. The story appears in seconds. Read it first yourself before reading it to your child — check the tone and pacing feel right.

5
Read it together slowly — point to the words

Sit beside your child. Read slowly. Point to each word as you say it. Use a calm, even voice. Pause after each sentence. You do not need to explain or quiz them. Simply reading together, repeatedly, is the whole activity.

Some children may want to hear the same story multiple times. This is completely fine — repetition is not a problem, it is part of how children with ASD process and feel safe with new information.
6
Offer one small choice — and only one

After the story, offer a single, simple choice: "Should the train go faster or slower in the next story?" or "Should the character's friend be a cat or a dog?" Small binary choices build decision-making confidence without overwhelming. Then generate the next story using their choice.

7
Build the story into a physical picture book

Go to bing.com/images/create. Describe one scene from the story. Generate an image. Print it. Repeat for each scene. Staple the pages together. Your child now has a picture book featuring their special interest as the hero. This physical object is something they can return to on difficult days.

Example image prompt for Bing
"A friendly blue steam train on a sunny track, surrounded by green hills, children's illustration style, soft colours, no scary elements"
Phase 3: Build a series and a routine
8
Save every story you generate

Copy each story into a Google Doc or Notes app. Number them: Story 1, Story 2, and so on. Over the summer you will build a personalised story library. On a stressful day, you can return to a story they already love rather than generating something new.

9
Ask Claude to create an audio version

Go to elevenlabs.io (free tier). Paste the story text. Choose a calm, gentle voice. Click Generate. Download the audio file. Play it at bedtime or during quiet time. Many children with ASD respond very well to hearing the same story as audio.

Keep the storytelling time consistent — same time of day, same location, same opening phrase. Predictability in the process reduces anxiety and makes your child more receptive to the content.
Activity 2Ages 5–15⏱ 15 min per story

AI-Generated Social Stories for Difficult Situations

When your child is about to face a situation that makes them anxious — a medical appointment, a new school, a family gathering — you can generate a personalised Social Story in minutes that prepares them for what to expect. This reduces meltdowns, anxiety, and resistance before difficult events.

How to create a Social Story for any situation
1
Identify the situation at least 3–4 days in advance

Social Stories work best when your child has time to hear them multiple times before the event. Don't generate one the night before — generate it a week ahead and read it daily.

2
Use this prompt in Claude
Social Story prompt — fill in the brackets
"Write a Social Story for a [age]-year-old child with autism who is anxious about [situation]. Write it in first person ('I will...'). Include: what will happen step by step, why it happens, what sounds or feelings they might notice, what they can do if they feel overwhelmed, and how they will feel when it is finished. Use very short sentences. Calm, reassuring tone. No surprises."
3
Personalise it further

After Claude generates the story, tell it: "Add a line about [their coping strategy, like squeezing a fidget toy or deep breathing]. Also, the child's name is [name]. Rewrite it using their name throughout." Now it's truly personal.

4
Read it once a day leading up to the event

Same time each day. Same calm voice. Same location if possible. After each reading, ask one simple question: "What will you do if you feel worried?" Let them answer in their own words or point to a picture.

5
Generate a picture to go with each scene

Use Bing Image Creator to illustrate the key moments. Print them and put them in order. Some children respond better to pictures than words — having both gives them options for how to process the story.

🔎 Example situations you can write Social Stories for right now: First haircut · Blood test or injection · Loud family gatherings (Eid, weddings) · Starting a new school term · Meeting a teacher for the first time · Flying on a plane · Fire alarm drills at school · Eating in a new place · Loud markets or malls
Activity 3Ages 6–14⏱ 30 min

Emotion Story Cards — Understanding Feelings Through Stories

Many children with ASD find it difficult to identify, name, and understand emotions — their own and others'. This activity uses AI to create short story cards showing characters experiencing different emotions, with simple explanations of what the emotion feels like in the body and what causes it.

1
Pick one emotion to focus on

Start with simpler emotions: happy, sad, frustrated, scared, proud, surprised. Do one per session — don't rush through multiple emotions at once.

2
Generate an emotion story with this prompt
Emotion story prompt
"Write a short story for a child with autism about a character who feels [emotion]. The character should love [special interest]. Describe: what happened to cause the emotion, what it feels like in the body (racing heart, tight chest, warm face, etc.), what the character did to express or manage it. Short sentences. Reassuring and clear."
3
Generate a face image for the emotion

Go to Bing Image Creator and type: "Simple cartoon face showing [emotion], gentle illustration style, no scary elements." Print it beside the story. Having a visual anchor for the feeling helps many children with ASD.

4
Make an emotions folder

Over the summer, build a folder of printed emotion story cards — one per emotion. Your child can refer to these cards when they are struggling to name what they feel. Point to the card and say: "Which one is this?"

Always validate emotions even when behaviour needs redirecting. "I can see you're frustrated. Frustration feels tight in your chest. Let's read the frustration story."

Before you start

Keeping Your Child Safe with AI Tools

AI tools are powerful. Like the internet, they come with risks. Here is what every parent needs to know — in plain language.

👤

Use your email for under-13s

Children under 13 should not create their own accounts on AI tools. Set up accounts using your email address and keep the login details yourself.

🚫

No personal information — ever

Teach your child: never type your full name, home address, school name, phone number, or photos of yourself into any AI chat. AI companies store conversation data.

🪑

Sit together the first few times

For children under 12, don't leave them alone with AI tools. Do the first three sessions together. You need to understand what they are doing before they do it independently.

🔍

Always fact-check one thing

AI sounds confident even when wrong. After every session, check one thing together. This single habit is more valuable than any activity in this guide.

📚

No AI for homework — clear rule

Set a household rule: AI can help them understand a topic and check their work. But the final submitted work must be written in their own words. Most teachers can detect AI-written text.

🕐

Set a session time limit

AI tools are engaging. Agree on a session length before you start — 30–45 minutes is a good guide. Use a kitchen timer or phone alarm. End on time, every time.

💬

Ask them to show you what they made

End every session by asking them to show you what they created or learned. This keeps you involved, reinforces learning, and lets them feel proud of their output.

🛡️

Safest tools to start with

Claude and Khan Academy are the safest starting points. Both have strong content moderation. Avoid giving younger children access to image generators without sitting beside them.

Parents ask us

Frequently Asked Questions

Real questions from non-technical parents. Click any question to read the answer.

My child isn't interested in technology. Should I still try this? ▼
Yes — because AI is no longer really "technology" in the way a remote control or a microwave is technology. It's a creative tool. The activities in this guide are built around what your child already loves: storytelling, drawing, music, sports, cooking. AI is the tool they use to express those interests, not the thing they have to be interested in. Start with whatever topic they love most and let AI serve that interest rather than replace it.
Will using AI make my child lazy or stop them thinking for themselves? ▼
Only if you use it lazily. A calculator doesn't make children bad at maths if they understand the problem they're calculating. AI is the same. If they use it to understand concepts, test ideas, and try things they couldn't do alone, it makes them more capable. If they just copy whatever it produces without thinking, it teaches nothing. The activities in this guide are specifically designed so your child is always directing the AI — not the other way around. The most important rule is: they should always read and question what the AI produces.
Is AI going to replace jobs by the time my child grows up? ▼
Some tasks within many jobs will be automated, yes. But history is very consistent on this: new technology creates more jobs than it removes — it changes which skills are valued. Children who understand how to use and think critically about AI will have an enormous advantage in any field they enter. The most useful thing you can do right now is help your child become fluent with these tools while teaching them to think about their limitations. That is exactly what this guide is for.
My child has ASD. Which activities should I start with? ▼
Start with the ASD Story Builder activities in the dedicated section above. Begin with the Personalised Story Series using their special interest — this is the lowest anxiety entry point. Read it together once a day for a week before trying anything else. Don't introduce multiple activities at once. The Social Stories activity (Activity 2) is the most immediately practical for managing day-to-day challenges. Add one activity at a time, keeping the routine and environment consistent each session. See the resources section for specialist support organisations in Pakistan and globally.
My child is very young (5–8). Is AI appropriate for them? ▼
With supervision, yes. The activities for ages 6–9 focus on creativity and imagination — making pictures, writing stories, asking questions about animals. These are completely normal childhood activities that AI can enhance. The key is that you sit beside them, help them type, and treat the session like reading a book together rather than leaving them in front of a screen. Never set up an account in their name — use your email. Think of it as a shared creative activity, not independent screen time.
I don't understand any of this technology. How can I help my child? ▼
You don't need to understand the technology — you need to be present and curious. When they get stuck, encourage them to ask the AI itself: "I'm trying to do X. How do I fix this?" The AI will explain in plain language. Your job is to ask good questions ("Do you think that's actually true?" "Why did you choose to do it that way?"), celebrate what they make, and help them think about whether the AI's output is actually good. You can do all of that without any technical knowledge. In fact, not knowing the answers often makes you a better learning partner — because you're genuinely curious alongside them.
What if my child uses AI to cheat on school assignments? ▼
This is worth addressing directly with your child before you start. Have a clear conversation: using AI to understand a topic, to check your work, or to get feedback is using it well. Submitting AI-written work as your own is dishonest and also counterproductive — they miss the learning and, increasingly, teachers and schools have tools to detect AI-written text. Set a household rule early and be explicit about it. If they're using AI well, there is no reason to hide it — encourage them to mention it when relevant. The habit of transparency with AI is one of the most important to build now.
Do I need to pay for any of these tools? ▼
For the vast majority of activities in this guide: no. Claude, Scratch, Khan Academy, Bing Image Creator, Canva (free tier), CapCut, Adobe Podcast, Replit, Poe.com, and Elements of AI are all free or have free tiers generous enough for everything listed here. The only paid options are iD Tech Camp and Udemy courses — both are optional extras for families who want more structure. You can complete every activity and most courses in this guide for zero cost.

How to talk to your kids about AI

Real questions children ask — with answers you can use at home. No tech degree required.

Kid asks
"What even IS AI?"
It's a computer program that learned by reading billions of texts — books, websites, conversations, articles. Now when you type a question, it predicts the most useful answer based on everything it has read. Think of it like a very well-read helper that never gets tired. But it doesn't actually understand anything — it recognises patterns really, really well. That's different from thinking.
Kid asks
"Can AI think like me?"
Not really. It can produce text that sounds like thinking, but it has no feelings, no experiences, and no opinions. It doesn't know what it feels like to be bored on a Sunday afternoon or nervous before an exam. It also doesn't remember our conversation once we close the tab. It's very impressive — but it's not a mind. It's a very sophisticated pattern-matching machine.
Kid asks
"Will AI take my future job?"
Some tasks in every job will be automated. But the history of technology is very clear: new tools create more jobs than they remove — they just change which skills are valued. The most secure future belongs to the kids who understand how to use AI thoughtfully, not the ones who ignore it. That's actually why we are doing this — to make sure you're one of them.
Kid asks
"Why does AI sometimes say wrong things?"
Because it generates answers based on patterns in text, not because it actually checked whether something is true. It is trying to predict what a correct answer looks like — and sometimes it gets it wrong while sounding completely confident. This is called "hallucination." That's why we always fact-check anything important. Knowing when not to trust AI is one of the most valuable things you can learn right now.
You (parent) wonder
"Is it safe for my child to use?"
With supervision and the right tools, yes. Start with Claude or Khanmigo — both are designed with safety, content moderation, and appropriate responses in mind. Sit with your child for the first few sessions. Teach them not to share personal information. Set a time limit. And treat it the way you'd treat any powerful tool: with guidance, clear rules, and curiosity. It becomes a very useful and safe skill over time.
